1. Welcome and Introduction

The Importance of Ecosystem Maintenance in Tasmania

Welcome to your studies in Conservation and Ecosystem Management. The island of Tasmania is a place of profound natural beauty and ecological significance, recognised globally for its unique and largely intact wild areas. From the rugged peaks of Cradle Mountain and the vast, silent button-grass plains of the South West, to the tranquil bays of the East Coast and the cool, temperate rainforests of the Tarkine, our ecosystems are our most valuable asset.

Key Point

Tasmania's isolation has resulted in unique evolutionary pathways, making its ecosystems particularly vulnerable to introduced species and habitat disturbance.

Tasmanian Ecosystems Flashcards

What is the most significant threat to Tasmanian ecosystems?

Invasive species, habitat loss and fragmentation, climate change, and pathogens are all significant threats, with invasive species often having the most immediate impact.

What percentage of Tasmania is protected in reserves?

Approximately 42% of Tasmania's land area is protected in reserves, one of the highest proportions of any state or territory in Australia.

What is the significance of Tasmania's Gondwanan heritage?

Tasmania contains many plant and animal lineages that date back to the supercontinent Gondwana, providing a living window into ancient ecosystems that have disappeared elsewhere.

2. Section 1: Prepare for Maintenance Activities

Thorough preparation is the single most important factor in the success of any ecosystem maintenance project. Rushing this stage leads to wasted effort, potential damage to the environment, and safety risks.

1.1 Identify and Confirm Maintenance Requirements of the Site

This is about understanding the "what," "why," and "how" of your task before you even set foot on the site.

The Planning Hierarchy: From Legislation to Daily Tasks

Maintenance work doesn't happen in a vacuum. It is part of a larger framework:

  • International Agreements: (e.g., Convention on Biological Diversity) influence national policy.
  • Commonwealth Legislation: (e.g., Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 - EPBC Act) protects matters of national environmental significance, like the World Heritage-listed Tasmanian Wilderness.
  • Tasmanian Legislation and Policy: This is the most direct layer of regulation. Key Acts include:
    • Nature Conservation Act 2002: Establishes the reserve system (National Parks, State Reserves) and provides for the management of flora and fauna.
    • Threatened Species Protection Act 1995: Provides for the listing and protection of threatened flora and fauna. Knowing the listed species in your work area is non-negotiable.
    • Weed Management Act 1999: Declares and categorises weeds (e.g., Declared Weeds), placing legal responsibilities on land managers to control them.
    • Forest Practices Act 1985: Regulates forestry operations on both public and private land, including requirements for biodiversity protection.
    • Aboriginal Heritage Act 1975: Protects Aboriginal cultural heritage. It is an offence to disturb, alter, or damage a relic or site.
  • Regional and Site-Specific Plans: This is where the broad legislation is translated into on-ground action.
    • Weed Management Plans: Outline priority weeds, control methods, monitoring protocols, and performance indicators for a specific area.
    • Fire Management Plans: Detail fuel reduction burning, asset protection, and ecological fire regimes. They define when, where, and how fire can be used as a management tool.
    • Revegetation Plans: Specify site preparation, species lists (emphasising local provenance seed), planting densities, and aftercare requirements.
    • Threatened Species Recovery Plans: Outline specific, targeted actions to protect and recover a listed species, which may include habitat management or restrictions on work timing.
  • Work Schedule/Instructions: Your daily or weekly tasking from your supervisor, which should be derived directly from the higher-level plans.
Legislation Flashcards

Which Tasmanian Act establishes the reserve system?

The Nature Conservation Act 2002 establishes Tasmania's reserve system including National Parks and State Reserves.

What is the purpose of the Threatened Species Protection Act 1995?

This Act provides for the listing and protection of threatened flora and fauna in Tasmania, ensuring their conservation and recovery.

What does the Weed Management Act 1999 do?

This Act declares and categorises weeds in Tasmania, placing legal responsibilities on land managers to control declared weeds.

Self-Directed Activity: Analysing a Management Plan

Go to the Tasmanian Parks and Wildlife Service website. Find the Management Plan for a reserve near you (e.g., Freycinet National Park, Peter Murrell Reserve). Read through the plan and answer the following:

  1. What are the key conservation values (e.g., specific ecosystems, threatened species, geomorphological features) identified for the area?
  2. List three significant threats to these values.
  3. What are two specific maintenance or management objectives mentioned in the plan?
  4. How does the plan address visitor access and its impact on the ecosystem?

1.2 Identify and Report Native Fauna and Flora and Significant Features

A core principle of ecological work is to "first, do no harm." This means being able to recognise and protect the valuable assets within your work site.

Tasmanian Ecosystems: A Primer

Tasmania's ecology is dominated by its cool, temperate climate, geological history, and complex topography. Key terrestrial ecosystems include:

  • Rainforests: Dense, damp forests dominated by myrtle beech (Nothofagus cunninghamii), sassafras (Atherosperma moschatum), and leatherwood (Eucryphia lucida). They are often rich in mosses, ferns, and lichens and are sensitive to fire and disturbance.
  • Eucalypt Forests: Ranging from tall wet eucalypt forests with Eucalyptus regnans (Swamp Gum, the world's tallest flowering plant) to drier forests with E. obliqua (Stringybark) and E. amygdalina (Black Peppermint). These forests are fire-adapted but vulnerable to high-intensity fires.
  • Alpine and Sub-alpine: Characterised by low-growing, hardy plants like cushion plants, snow gums (E. coccifera), and pencil pines (Athrotaxis cupressoides) on the Central Plateau. These areas are extremely sensitive to trampling and climate change.
  • Buttongrass Moorlands: A vast, fire-adapted ecosystem in the South West, dominated by Gymnoschoenus sphaerocephalus, and home to unique fauna like the ground parrot. They are nutrient-poor systems.
  • Coastal Heathlands: Wind-sheared, shrub-dominated communities on coastal dunes and headlands, featuring banksias, tea-trees, and correas. They are crucial for stabilising soils and provide habitat for many invertebrates and birds.
Self-Directed Activity: Natural Values Atlas Investigation
  1. Access the Tasmanian Government's Natural Values Atlas (NVA).
  2. Search for your local suburb or a nearby reserve.
  3. Generate a report and identify:
    • Two threatened plant species listed for the area.
    • Two threatened animal species.
    • One "significant community" (e.g., a specific type of forest or wetland).
  4. For one of the threatened species, research its specific habitat requirements and key identifying features. Present your findings in a short paragraph.

1.3 Identify Occupational Health and Safety Hazards and Report to Supervisor

Your safety, and the safety of your colleagues, is paramount. The remote and rugged nature of much of Tasmania's landscape makes risk management non-negotiable.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

PPE is your last line of defence. Standard PPE for field work includes:

  • Sturdy boots (steel-capped, ankle support)
  • Long pants and long-sleeved shirt (to protect from sun, scratches, and insects)
  • Safety glasses
  • Gloves (appropriate for the task, e.g., leather for handling woody weeds, chemical-resistant for spraying)
  • Hearing protection (for power tools)
  • High-visibility clothing (if working near tracks or roads)
Self-Directed Activity: Developing a Job Safety Analysis (JSA)

Develop a detailed JSA for the task of manually removing gorse (using loppers and handsaws) in an area with rough, uneven terrain and a dense understorey that includes native laurels.

Use a table with the following headings:

Job Step Hazard Risk Control Measures
Travel to site Uneven ground, hidden holes Tripping, falls, sprains Walk carefully, use a pole to probe ground, wear sturdy ankle-supporting boots.
Cutting gorse Sharp thorns, repetitive strain, native laurel sap Cuts, scratches, muscular injury, severe skin and eye irritation Wear leather gloves, long sleeves, and safety glasses. Use correct lifting/cutting techniques, take regular breaks. Be aware of nearby laurels; avoid contacting sap.
Moving debris Thorns, heavy lifting, uneven terrain Cuts, back injury, trips Wear gloves, use correct manual handling techniques (bend knees, keep back straight), clear a safe path.
... ... ... ...

Complete the JSA for all steps, including tool transport, work, and clean-up.

3. Section 2: Undertake Maintenance Activities

This section covers the practical application of your knowledge and skills. It is the "on-ground" phase where planning meets action. The emphasis here is on executing tasks effectively, efficiently, and with minimal negative impact on the very environment you are working to maintain.

2.1 Carry out Maintenance of the Native Ecosystem According to the Maintenance Plan and Supervisory Requirements

This performance criterion is about implementing the plan with precision and adhering to instructions. It requires a blend of technical skill, ecological understanding, and disciplined work practices.

Weed Control Methods Flashcards

What is the "cut and paint" method?

Cutting a woody weed close to the ground and immediately applying herbicide to the cut surface to prevent regrowth.

What is the "drill and fill" method?

Drilling holes into the trunk of a woody weed and filling them with herbicide, used for larger trees where cutting isn't practical.

When is foliar spraying appropriate?

For controlling seedlings or herbaceous weeds, but must be used carefully to avoid damage to non-target plants.

Self-Directed Activity: Weed Control Strategy Report

Choose a specific Tasmanian environmental weed (e.g., Spanish Heath, English Broom, Willow). Research and write a 500-word report that outlines:

  1. Identification: Key features to identify it.
  2. Ecology: Its life cycle, reproduction, and dispersal method.
  3. Impact: The specific threat it poses to Tasmanian ecosystems.
  4. Control Strategy: A detailed, integrated plan for its control, including at least two different methods (e.g., manual and chemical), the best timing for control, and how you would dispose of the waste material.

2.2 Minimise Damage to the Environment During Maintenance Work

This is the principle of "first, do no harm" in practice. The methods used to maintain an ecosystem must not be more damaging than the problem they are solving.

Key Point

Always consider the timing of maintenance activities to avoid disturbing breeding wildlife or damaging sensitive vegetation during vulnerable growth periods.

Self-Directed Activity: Case Study - Protecting a Sensitive Site

Scenario: You have been tasked with controlling a patch of gorse (Ulex europaeus) that is invading a healthy patch of native heathland. Within the heathland, you have identified several populations of the vulnerable orchid Caladenia tonellii (Robust Fingers) and you know the soil is potentially infected with Phytophthora cinnamomi.

Task: Develop a step-by-step work procedure that details how you will control the gorse while:

  • Preventing the spread of Phytophthora.
  • Ensuring no damage to the native heathland vegetation or the orchids.
  • Ensuring the safety of the work crew.

Your procedure should specify tools, methods, hygiene protocols, and any timing considerations.

2.3 Select, Use and Maintain Tools and Equipment

The right tool, used correctly and kept in good condition, is fundamental to safe and efficient work.

Self-Directed Activity: Tool Maintenance Log

Create a digital or physical "Tool Maintenance Logbook." For one week, practice using and maintaining a set of hand tools (e.g., loppers, handsaw, mattock). After each use, log the following:

  • Date and Tool Used.
  • Task it was used for.
  • Condition before use (e.g., sharp, clean, loose handle?).
  • Maintenance performed after use (e.g., cleaned with wire brush, washed, dried, oiled, sharpened).
  • Any repairs needed.

This habit is a cornerstone of professional practice.

Quiz 2: Practical Application

2. You need to control weeds along a creek bank that is a known habitat for the Giant Freshwater Lobster. What specific precautions must you take when selecting and applying a herbicide?
3. Explain the term "local provenance" in the context of revegetation. Why is it a critical principle in Tasmanian ecosystem restoration?

4. Section 3: Complete Maintenance Activities

The final stage of the work process is often the most neglected but is equally as important as the preparation and execution. Proper completion ensures the long-term success of the work, the safety of the site, and provides valuable data for future management.

3.1 Reinstate Site According to the Maintenance Plan

Reinstatement is about actively assisting the natural recovery of the site after your intervention.

Site Reinstatement Flashcards

What is the purpose of site reinstatement?

To assist natural recovery, prevent erosion, reduce weed invasion, and restore ecological function after maintenance activities.

What are three methods for erosion control on disturbed sites?

1. Mulching with organic material
2. Installing erosion control matting
3. Revegetating with appropriate native species

Why is monitoring important after maintenance work?

To assess the effectiveness of treatments, identify any issues requiring follow-up, and inform future management decisions.

Self-Directed Activity: Site Reinstatement Plan

Using the same scenario from the previous activity (gorse control in a heathland with orchids and Phytophthora risk), now develop a reinstatement plan. Detail:

  1. What will you do with the cut gorse material? (Justify your choice based on biosecurity and ecological principles).
  2. How will you treat the soil that has been disturbed around the gorse plants to prevent erosion and weed invasion?
  3. What measures, if any, will you put in place to encourage the native heathland to recolonise the area?
  4. How will you mark the area for future monitoring?

3.2 Dispose of All Waste Material

Responsible waste disposal closes the loop on your work activity and is a legal and ethical requirement.

Key Point

Always follow the hierarchy of waste management: avoid creating waste where possible, then reduce, reuse, recycle, and only as a last resort, dispose of waste appropriately.

Self-Directed Activity: Waste Management Flowchart

Create a single-page, visual flowchart to guide decision-making for waste disposal on a conservation worksite. It should have "decision diamonds" asking questions like:

  • "Is the material a declared weed that can resprout or set seed?"
  • "Is the material suitable for habitat?"
  • "Is the material a chemical container?"

This will provide a clear, at-a-glance guide for you and your team.

3.3 Clean and Store Tools and Equipment

This routine is vital for the longevity of equipment, cost-control for your organisation, and safety for the next user.

3.4 Record and Report Work Outcomes

If it wasn't recorded, it wasn't done. Documentation provides the evidence of your work, informs future management, and is essential for accountability.

Self-Directed Activity: Writing a Professional Field Report

Using the following scenario, write a full and professional field report.

Scenario: On November 15th, 2024, you and a colleague (Jane Doe) spent the day at the "North-South Track" in the Peter Murrell Reserve. The task was to control broom seedlings in a 50m x 50m area that was treated for mature broom last year. The weather was fine and sunny, 18°C. You used a knapsack sprayer with a 1% solution of Glyphosate (as per the product label) and treated approximately 250 seedlings. You noticed several native Correa alba plants flowering healthily within the treatment area and took care to avoid spraying them. At 2:15 PM, Jane slightly twisted her ankle on a hidden hole but was able to walk back to the vehicle. You finished at 3:30 PM, cleaned all equipment, and left the site tidy. You recommend a monitoring visit in 6 months to check for any missed plants.

Structure your report with clear headings (e.g., Site Details, Personnel, Work Summary, Observations, Incidents, Recommendations).

Appendix B: Common Tasmanian Weeds and Control Methods

Weed Key Identification Impact Recommended Control Methods
Gorse (Ulex europaeus) Dense, spiny shrub, yellow flowers. Smothers native vegetation, forms impenetrable thickets. Cut and paint, foliar spray (for seedlings), biological control (gorse spider mite).
Broom (Cytisus scoparius) Tall shrub, green ribbed stems, bright yellow flowers. Forms dense stands, fixes nitrogen altering soil chemistry. Cut and paint, foliar spray, hand-pull seedlings.
Spanish Heath (Erica lusitanica) Fine-leaved shrub, small white/pink flowers. Invades heathlands and grasslands, forms monocultures. Cut and paint, foliar spray.
Arum Lily (Zantedeschia aethiopica) Large, arrow-shaped leaves, white flower spathe. Invades damp areas, replaces native riparian vegetation. Dig out tubers, foliar spray.
Willows (Salix spp.) Trees, long thin leaves. Invades waterways, destabilises banks, shades out natives. Stem injection, cut and paint.

  • Trees: Eucalyptus globulus (Blue Gum), Eucalyptus ovata (Black Gum), Eucalyptus viminalis (White Gum), Acacia melanoxylon (Blackwood), Allocasuarina verticillata (Drooping She-oak).
  • Shrubs: Acacia dealbata (Silver Wattle), Bursaria spinosa (Prickly Box), Correa alba (White Correa), Leptospermum scoparium (Manuka), Melaleuca squarrosa (Scented Paperbark).
